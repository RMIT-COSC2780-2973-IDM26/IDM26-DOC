# Academic Integrity

Being an advanced course, students are expected to be fully aware of academic integrity and to behave **professionally** and **ethically** at all times. This is not only a university requirement, it is a core expectation of professional practice.

You may **not** collude with any other individual or plagiarise work, whether from classmates, past students, online sources, or AI tools.

Students are encouraged to discuss assignment topics at a **conceptual and verbal level**, but _all submitted work must represent the individual's own understanding and effort_. You are expected to present the results of **your own thinking, problem solving, and coding** 🫵.

Suspected collusion or plagiarism will be handled according to the [RMIT Academic Integrity Policy](https://www.rmit.edu.au/students/student-essentials/rights-and-responsibilities/academic-integrity) and the guidelines provided in this course.

**Remember:** if a solution is “out there”, the teaching staff also has access to it. 👁️

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Most students understand the difference between helping others learn and **cheating** (or helping others cheat). Plagiarism is cheating and is treated as a serious academic offence.
>
> If academic misconduct occurs, we will pursue the **strongest consequences available under RMIT regulations**.


## Why Academic Integrity Matters (Beyond University Rules)

Assessments and projects exist for one main reason:

🫵 **to help you learn and develop skills**.

If you submit work that is not genuinely yours --- even if you are never caught!! --- you are ultimately harming **yourself**:

- You do not develop the reasoning and technical skills the course is designed to build.
- You train yourself into **unethical professional habits**.
- You graduate with credentials that do not match your actual abilities.
- You enter the workforce unprepared to solve real problems independently.

In today's world, this matters more than ever. ❗

Routine coding, writing, or problem solving can increasingly be performed by **AI systems**. The value of a university graduate lies not in producing answer that pass the assessment, but in  **understanding**, **reasoning**, **designing solutions**, and **taking responsibility** for decisions.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> If your work can be replaced by AI because you never developed genuine expertise, then your professional role can also be replaced.

Cheating is therefore not a shortcut: it is **self-sabotage**. 👎

**As in sport:** you cannot perform well in competition if you did not truly train. There are no shortcuts to competence. 🥇 ⚽ 🏏 ⛹️‍♂️ 🏊

So, for your own benefit:

- Do not trick yourself.  🙅
- Do not become a cheater. 👎🏼
- Do not give up the trust of others; it is often impossible to recover it back. 🤝
- Do not help being useless and replaceable. 🤷
- Do not miss this unique opportunity to learn; most people in the world do not have it. If anything, do it for them. 🙏

> [!CAUTION]
> Academic integrity rules are **not a game to be defeated**. Attempts to circumvent policies (including misuse of collaboration, external solutions, or AI tools) will be treated as plagiarism and academic misconduct. 👎


## Practical Academic Integrity Guidelines

Please keep the following principles in mind when working on assessments:


1. 🛑 **Do not look at solutions produced by others or generated by AI tools**, whether inside the course (e.g., classmates) or outside the course (e.g., websites, repositories, forums, or AI assistants).

   Other people have already completed *their* learning journey: **this is your 🫵 opportunity to develop your own skills and understanding**.
   The safest rule is simple: **if you do not look at solutions, you cannot accidentally misuse them.**
2. 🛑 **Do not copy, reuse, or rely on another student's work or AI-generated output**, even if someone explains it to you first.

   Likewise, **do not share or show your written solutions or code to others**.
   Collaboration is encouraged **only at a verbal and conceptual level**. Discuss ideas, approaches, and intuition, but keep implementations and written work strictly individual.
3. 🛑 **Do not copy solutions (fully or partially) from the web.**

   An assignment is **not** an open-source project. Searching online forums, repositories, or AI systems for solutions defeats the purpose of the assessment.

   The safest approach is to **avoid searching the web or AI systems for solutions altogether**, and instead build your answers from first principles.

   We use code similarity detection tools across current submissions, past offerings, and publicly available online code. We trust you will act professionally and respect the learning process.
4. 🛑 **Modifying or adapting someone else's solution (including AI outputs!) does *not* make it your own work.**

   You are expected to generate solutions independently.

   Reusing *auxiliary* material is acceptable (e.g., a standard routine to sort numbers), provided that:
   - you fully understand the reused code, and
   - you clearly acknowledge its source.
5. ✅ **Studying algorithms and techniques is encouraged.**

   Reading pseudo-code or conceptual explanations from textbooks, research papers, lecture slides, or reputable learning resources is completely appropriate and expected (e.g., studying the pseudo-code for minimax or A*).

   Learning concepts is encouraged but copying implementations is not.
6. ✅ **When in doubt, ask the teaching staff.**

   If you are unsure whether something is acceptable, please ask. Seeking clarification demonstrates professionalism and academic maturity, and we are always happy to help. ✋

> [!IMPORTANT]
> The aim of the course and this project is **your learning** 🫵🥇. Everything we are teaching here and you are learning is NOT new and has been in textbooks for decades. Again, the objective is not for you to discover anything new, but to **help you learn** and acquire skills that will be useful for you as a Computer Scientist. So, as said, **don't trick yourself** and see how far you can reach by developing _your_ solutions to all assessments. It is NOT necessary to solve everything or to solve it to perfection, but enough to confidence us that you got the crux of it. 😄

## Forum postings on assignment

Do not ever post any information on the forum that may disclose how to solve a question or what the solution may be. You can only post assignment related questions for **clarification** on what is being asked, for auxiliary programming tasks (e.g., how to sort a list of numbers in Python), or for generic issues and problems with the techniques studied in the course. Posts  discussing possible solutions or strategies may directly be considered plagiarism, see above. If in doubt, do not post and ask your question to the lecturer or tutor instead (remember EdStem allows private posting).
